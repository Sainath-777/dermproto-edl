The core problem being solved

Rare diseases have almost no labeled images — sometimes single digits per condition. Standard deep learning needs thousands of examples per class, so it simply cannot be trained the normal way here.

The core idea, in one sentence

Borrow a backbone that already understands general images (DINOv2), compare a new photo to a handful of references using distance (Prototypical Networks), and wrap the whole decision in a confidence estimate that can honestly say "not sure" (Evidential Deep Learning).

What makes this a thesis-worthy system

Each of the three ideas exists in prior work separately. The novel piece — covered in Part 4 and 5 — is fusing support-set dispersion into the evidence computation itself, so uncertainty reflects not just "how far is the query" but "how trustworthy was the reference set."

Part 1

CNN Basics — how a machine "sees" an image at all

ELI5: a CNN slides tiny pattern-detectors across a photo, then stacks the detections into bigger and bigger patterns — edges become textures, textures become shapes, shapes become objects.

What's actually happening

A convolution is a small grid of numbers (a filter) slid across every position of the image, multiplying and summing as it goes. One filter might light up wherever it sees a vertical edge; another lights up for a certain color blob.

Pooling just shrinks the resulting grid, keeping only the strongest response in each small region — this makes the network care less about exact pixel position and more about "was this pattern present somewhere around here."

Why it exists — what breaks without it

If you fed raw pixels straight into a plain fully-connected layer, the network would need a separate weight for every pixel-to-pixel relationship — millions of parameters, and it would treat a cat's ear in the top-left as a totally different pattern than the same ear in the bottom-right.

Convolution reuses the same small filter everywhere in the image, so it detects "an edge" regardless of where it appears. This is what makes CNNs trainable on images at all.

Where this fits in our pipeline

We do not train a CNN from scratch for the disease classes — we don't have the thousands of images that requires. But every idea here (filters detecting patterns, stacking simple→complex features) is the ancestor of DINOv2 in Part 2, which uses a different internal design (transformer, not convolution) but solves the exact same underlying job: turn pixels into meaningful features.

Part 2

DINOv2 — the frozen feature extractor (backbone)

ELI5: DINOv2 is a pre-trained "master eye" that already learned what images generally look like, by studying millions of unlabeled photos and being trained to recognize the same photo under different crops and lighting.

What it is

A tensor is just a grid of numbers with some number of dimensions — a photo is a 3D tensor (height × width × color channels). An embedding is what you get after squeezing all that raw pixel information down into a much shorter list of numbers (e.g. 768) that captures the image's meaning rather than its raw pixels — visually or semantically similar images land near each other in this "latent space."

Self-attention lets every image patch look at every other patch and decide how relevant they are to each other — this is how the model figures out that a lesion's texture depends on the surrounding skin, not just the pixels directly under it.

Why it exists — what breaks without it

DINOv2 was trained self-supervised — no human labels at all. It was shown different crops/views of the same unlabeled image and trained to produce consistent embeddings for both. Across millions of images, this forces it to learn general-purpose visual structure.

Backbone freezing: once loaded, we lock DINOv2's weights and never update them again. With only 5 images per disease, letting a huge network keep adjusting would make it instantly memorize those 5 photos rather than learn the disease pattern — this is catastrophic overfitting. Freezing forces all the real learning to happen in the small heads downstream.

Where this fits in our pipeline

This is Stage 1 of the real pipeline. Every one of our 5 reference photos, and the 1 new query photo, gets pushed through this exact frozen DINOv2 and comes out as a single embedding vector. From this point on, nothing downstream ever looks at raw pixels again — only these vectors.

ELI5: instead of training on one giant fixed task, the model practices thousands of tiny "pop quizzes" — each one just a handful of example photos plus one new photo to classify — so it gets good at the *skill* of learning from few examples.

Our real diagnosis scenario is exactly one episode: N=1 disease class, K=5 support (reference) photos, and a query set of 1 new photo. The model was trained on thousands of episodes shaped exactly like this, just using different, more common diseases as stand-ins during training — so it has effectively "practiced this exact situation" thousands of times before ever seeing the real rare disease.

What it is

The support set is the labeled reference photos you're allowed to look at (our 5 photos). The query set is the new, unlabeled photo(s) you must classify using only what you saw in the support set (our 1 new photo).

Data augmentation (random crops, flips, color jitter) is applied to the support images to synthetically stretch 5 real photos into a more varied, more representative sample of what that disease can look like.

Why it exists — what breaks without it

Ordinary training assumes you'll see a class thousands of times before being tested on it. Real deployment here breaks that assumption completely — the rare disease at test time may have never been seen during training at all.

Episodic training fixes this mismatch by making every training step already look like the real test: few examples in, one new example to classify. The model never gets the luxury of memorizing a class — it's forced to learn a general strategy for comparing few examples to a new one.

Where this fits in our pipeline

This defines the shape of every training step, and it's also literally the shape of inference. Once embeddings exist for the 5 support photos and 1 query photo (Part 2's output), Part 4 (Prototypical Networks) is what actually solves this specific episode.

Part 4

Prototypical Networks — classifying by distance, not by weights

ELI5: average your 5 reference photos into one "typical" point in embedding space (the prototype), then measure how far the new photo lands from that point. Close = same disease. Far = probably not.

★ Your novel contribution — support-set dispersion (σk)

The gap in standard Prototypical Networks: the original method treats every prototype as equally trustworthy. A prototype built from 5 nearly-identical reference photos gets the exact same weight as one built from 5 wildly inconsistent-looking photos. For rare diseases — where presentation genuinely varies a lot between patients — that's a blind spot: a "loose," scattered reference set is a much shakier basis for a confident diagnosis than a "tight" one, and the standard architecture has no way to express that difference.

The fix: alongside the prototype (the mean of the 5 support embeddings), also compute their dispersion σk — how spread out those 5 embeddings are around their own average (e.g. their variance, or the average pairwise distance between them).

Intuition: distance-to-query tells you about the query. Dispersion tells you about how much to trust the reference set itself. A close distance means little if σk is high — the "close match" might just be luck against a noisy, disagreeing set of references. Fusing both is what lets the final uncertainty score reflect evidence reliability, not just raw closeness.

What it is

A prototype is nothing fancy — literally the coordinate-wise average of the support embeddings for one class. Euclidean distance is the plain straight-line distance between two vectors, same idea as distance between two points on a map, just in a higher-dimensional space.

Why it exists — what breaks without it

Ordinary classifiers learn a fixed decision boundary per class from thousands of examples. With 5 examples, there isn't enough data to learn a boundary — but there is enough to compute a reasonable average position. Metric-based comparison sidesteps the "not enough data to learn weights" problem entirely by turning classification into geometry instead.

Where this fits in our pipeline

This is Stage 2 of the real pipeline, right after embeddings exist. Its two outputs — distance and σk — are exactly the two inputs the Evidential Head in Part 5 needs to produce a trustworthy confidence score.

Part 5

Evidential Deep Learning — turning distance into honest confidence

ELI5: instead of forcing the model to always output "I'm 100% sure it's one of these," it collects "evidence" for each class, and if there just isn't much evidence anywhere, it openly says so — that's the mechanism behind "ask a doctor."

evidence: e_k = f(distance_k, σ_k) — e_k ≥ 0, low distance + low σ_k → high e_k Dirichlet: α_k = e_k + 1 total mass: S = Σ_k α_k probability: p_k = α_k / S uncertainty: u = K / S (K = number of classes)

What it is

Evidence ek is an amount, not a probability — it can be genuinely near-zero if nothing supports that class. A Dirichlet distribution is a "distribution over distributions": instead of outputting one fixed probability per class, the model outputs parameters (αk) describing a whole range of plausible probability distributions — how tight or spread that range is directly tells you the model's confidence.

The uncertainty score u = K/S is elegantly simple: when there's zero real evidence anywhere, S collapses to just K (the "+1" priors), making u = 1 — maximum uncertainty. As real evidence accumulates for some class, S grows and u shrinks toward 0.

Why it exists — what breaks without it

A normal softmax output is forced to sum to 100% no matter what — even total nonsense input gets some confident-looking probability distribution out. Softmax has no way to express "I found nothing convincing." EDL fixes this by making the total "mass" itself variable (via S), so genuinely weak evidence produces genuinely high uncertainty instead of a falsely confident guess.

KL-divergence regularization adds a loss term that specifically shrinks evidence generated for the wrong classes toward zero, without punishing evidence for the right one — this is what teaches the model to be selectively confident instead of just maximizing evidence everywhere. KL annealing ramps this penalty up slowly over training, because applying it at full strength from step one would just teach the model to output zero evidence for everything — permanently "unsure" and useless. It has to learn to classify first, then learn humility.

Where this fits in our pipeline

This is Stage 3, the final scoring stage. It's also where your novel σk signal actually pays off: it doesn't just adjust the predicted class, it directly shapes ek, which directly shapes u — meaning the uncertainty score genuinely reflects "was the reference set itself reliable," not only "was the query close."

Part 6

Full pipeline walkthrough — tracing the running example

5 reference photos of a rare skin condition, 1 new photo to diagnose — followed from raw pixels to final decision.

1

Raw input. 5 reference photos (support set, K=5) of the rare skin condition, plus 1 new photo (query) whose diagnosis is unknown. All 6 images are just pixel grids so far.

2

Frozen DINOv2 backbone. Each of the 6 images is patchified, passed through self-attention transformer blocks, and reduced to one embedding vector each. Backbone weights never change — no learning happens here, only feature extraction. Output: 5 support embeddings + 1 query embedding.

3

Prototype + dispersion. The 5 support embeddings are averaged into one prototype vector ck for this disease, and their spread around that average is measured as σk. A tight cluster of 5 references → low σk → this reference set is trustworthy. A scattered cluster → high σk → treat matches against it with caution.

4

Query distance. The Euclidean distance between the query embedding and the prototype ck is computed — how far is this new photo from "typical" for this disease.

5

Evidential head. Distance and σk are combined into evidence ek. This becomes a Dirichlet parameter αk = ek + 1, and combined across all candidate classes into S = Σαk.

6

Output. Predicted probability pk = αk/S and uncertainty u = K/S are produced together.

7

Final decision. If u is below the chosen threshold, the system outputs the diagnosis with its confidence score. If u is above the threshold — whether because the query was genuinely far from the prototype, or because the 5 reference photos disagreed with each other too much — the system refuses to guess and defers: "not confident enough, ask a doctor."

Part 7

Evaluation & Clinical Metrics Layer

How you prove the uncertainty score is actually trustworthy, not just decorative.

Selective classification

Instead of forcing an answer for every input, the system is allowed a "reject / refer to human" option whenever u exceeds a threshold. Evaluated by plotting accuracy-vs-coverage: how much does accuracy improve as you allow the system to reject more cases?

Expected Calibration Error (ECE)

Checks whether stated confidence matches real accuracy — if the model says "90% confident" a thousand times, was it actually right about 90% of those times? Low ECE = trustworthy confidence numbers.

Macro F1-score

Averages the F1-score of every class equally, regardless of how many support examples each disease has. Critical here because rare-disease datasets are extremely imbalanced — a plain accuracy number would be dominated by whichever class has more data.

Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection

Tests the system on images that don't belong to any trained disease category at all (e.g. a normal mole, or an unrelated photo). The uncertainty score doubles as a natural OOD detector: a genuinely novel input should also produce low evidence and high u, without needing a separate mechanism.
